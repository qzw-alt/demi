Macao's Health Tourism Expands with New Hospitals and TCM Routes: How Does Mainland China Compare?

As Macao opens Peking Union Medical College Hospital and cross-border wellness routes, the region's "tourism plus big health" model is getting serious, but mainland China has been building for years.

Published: April 14, 2026  |  By China Hospitals Guide  |  Category: Medical Tourism

The Breaking News

Macao is stepping up its game in health tourism, backed by mainland Chinese medical infrastructure. At MITE 2026 (April 8-12, 2026), the Macao Government Tourism Office unveiled a wave of new medical and wellness facilities designed to position the city as a credible international health destination. The key announcements, as reported by TTG Asia on April 13:

  • The Islands Healthcare Complex – Macao Medical Center of Peking Union Medical College Hospital, a first-time MITE exhibitor showing off smart healthcare innovations and high-end medical equipment, with the hospital explicitly targeting both mainland Chinese patients and overseas Chinese from Southeast Asia and beyond.
  • iRad Hospital at Studio City Macau, a new diagnostic and radiology-focused facility within the Studio City integrated resort complex.
  • Medical Center at Wynn Palace Cotai, adding clinical services to one of Macao's flagship luxury properties.
  • The Traditional Chinese Medicine Technology and Creativity Museum, a dedicated TCM cultural and educational venue.

On April 11, the Macao-Hengqin Wellness Traveller Forum drew 150 delegates to the TCM Cultural Experience Centre in Hengqin, unveiling new cross-border medical tourism routes spanning both Macao and Hengqin over two to three days. The forum was a collaboration between Macao's Economic Development Bureau and the Hengqin authority, a clear signal that Macao's health tourism strategy is tightly integrated with mainland China's Greater Bay Area plan.

Xue Ji, assistant sales manager of the TCM Cultural Experience Centre, told TTG Asia that the centre is actively courting Hong Kong students, with some 1,600 expected to join TCM study programmes from April through June 2026 alone.

Why this matters: Macao is drawing on mainland China's medical infrastructure (Peking Union Medical College, TCM heritage, Greater Bay Area connectivity) under a tourism brand. For international patients considering China as a medical destination, this convergence of top mainland hospitals, integrated TCM, and regional logistics deserves a close look.

Macao's "Tourism Plus Big Health" Vision

Macao's push isn't new, but the 2026 expansion is a qualitative leap. The MGTO's language has shifted from general wellness promotion to concrete infrastructure delivery:

Cindia Lam, dean of the Institute of Executive and Professional Development at the Macao University of Tourism, described the model that works best for Macao: a broader health and wellness experience combining medical or wellness services with hospitality, therapeutic dining, TCM elements, and selected cultural and leisure offerings. She stressed that success requires collaboration across three groups: hospitals for clinical quality, tourism operators for visitor experience, and government for policy coordination.

Andy Wu, managing director of Gam Lun Macau Tours, sees growing opportunity for travel agents to partner with Macao's integrated resorts as more diversify into medical and wellness services. The link between hospital-grade clinical infrastructure and five-star hospitality is Macao's differentiator, and mainland China has been slower to package something similar for international visitors.

Mainland China's Health Tourism Picture

While Macao was announcing new facilities at MITE 2026, mainland China was simultaneously advancing its own parallel health tourism ecosystem, one substantially larger in scale, deeper in clinical capability, and broader in geographic reach.

China officially recorded 1.28 million inbound international patients in 2025, a 30% jump from 2024, according to official figures. The nine-ministry policy framework released in April 2026 is actively promoting medical tourism with coordinated visa improvements, investment incentives, and international patient service standards at major hospitals in Shanghai, Beijing, Guangzhou, and Hainan.

For international patients, and for Hong Kong, Macau, and overseas Chinese patients in particular, mainland China has structural advantages that Macao cannot easily replicate at scale:

  • Peking Union Medical College Hospital (PUMCH) in Beijing is consistently ranked among China's top hospitals and is the national reference centre for complex and rare conditions, something the PUMCH satellite in Macao cannot fully replicate.
  • Hainan's Boao Lecheng international medical tourism zone offers legally enabled access to treatments and drugs not yet approved on the mainland, including for certain cancers and regenerative medicine applications. This is a real advantage for patients looking for advanced options.
  • Traditional Chinese Medicine integration: mainland China's TCM hospitals have decades of clinical research infrastructure behind them. Institutions like Guang'anmen Hospital in Beijing and Shanghai Yueyang Hospital offer evidence-based TCM integrated with modern medicine at a scale and specialisation that standalone TCM museums cannot match.
  • Proton therapy and advanced radiotherapy: China now operates multiple dedicated proton therapy centres in Shanghai, Guangzhou, and elsewhere, at costs typically 30-60% below equivalent treatment in Western countries.
  • Scale and depth of specialty: China performs some of the highest volumes of complex procedures globally, including cardiac surgery, orthopaedic implants, and oncology, giving clinical teams an exceptional learning curve and experience base.

The Macao-Hengqin integration points to a larger reality. The Greater Bay Area is emerging as a single medical destination where patients can combine mainland clinical depth with regional hospitality and travel experiences. Patients treated at PUMCH in Beijing or Guangzhou can recover in Hong Kong or Macao, or vice versa.

Key Takeaways

  • Macao's 2026 health tourism expansion is real and getting more serious: new hospitals, TCM infrastructure, and cross-border Macao-Hengqin routes signal genuine intent. This is no longer just wellness brochures.
  • But Macao's clinical depth has limits: for diagnostics, wellness recovery, and hospitality-integrated minor procedures, Macao is compelling. For complex conditions like advanced cancer, major cardiac surgery, or rare diseases, mainland China's top hospitals have decades of scale that Macao's new facilities cannot yet match.
  • Greater Bay Area integration is the real story: Macao's health tourism push is most powerful when viewed as part of the GBA medical ecosystem, where patients can access mainland clinical depth with Macao's hospitality layer. This combined model is genuinely unique in Asia.
  • China's TCM argument is stronger on the mainland: the TCM Cultural Experience Centre in Hengqin is a gateway, but mainland China's TCM hospitals offer clinical TCM (integrated oncology, rehabilitation, chronic disease management) backed by research and regulatory frameworks.
  • For international patients, the question is what you need treated: Macao for wellness and diagnostics with comfort; mainland China for serious, complex, or advanced treatments at accessible prices.
