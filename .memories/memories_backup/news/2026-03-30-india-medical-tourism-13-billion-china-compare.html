India's Medical Tourism Boom: $13 Billion and Counting — How Does China Compare?

📅 March 30, 2026 ✍️ China Hospitals Guide 🏥 Medical Tourism

India's medical tourism industry is surging to $13 billion in 2026, fueled by government investment in five new dedicated medical hubs. As the global healthcare landscape shifts, how does China stack up as a destination for international patients seeking quality care at accessible prices?

🔥 The Breaking News: India's $13 Billion Medical Tourism Surge

India's medical tourism sector is projected to reach $13 billion in 2026, cementing its position as one of the world's premier destinations for affordable, high-quality healthcare. Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman's 2026 Budget announcement solidified this growth trajectory with the designation of five new medical tourism hubs across the country.

The hubs—strategically located in cities with established healthcare infrastructure—aim to streamline the medical travel experience. International patients will benefit from faster visa processing, dedicated international patient wings, and coordinated care packages that bundle treatment with accommodation and travel assistance.

This growth comes despite geopolitical turbulence. The Iran conflict has disrupted traditional medical travel routes from the Middle East, redirecting thousands of patients toward India as a stable, trusted alternative. Industry experts note that India's combination of English-language healthcare, JCI-accredited hospitals, and significant cost advantages makes it uniquely positioned to capture this shifting demand.

Top treatment categories driving India's medical tourism include cardiac surgery, orthopedics, cancer treatment, fertility procedures, and cosmetic surgeries. Major hospital chains like Apollo, Fortis, and Manipal have expanded their international patient divisions, with some reporting 30-40% year-over-year growth in foreign patient volumes.

🏥 China's Current Landscape: A Rising Contender

While India dominates headlines, China has quietly built a formidable medical tourism sector of its own. According to the National Health Commission, Chinese hospitals treated approximately 1.28 million international patients in the past year—a remarkable 73.6% increase from three years prior.

China's approach differs strategically. Rather than positioning itself primarily as a low-cost option, China emphasizes its cutting-edge technology and specialized expertise in complex conditions. Key advantages include:

  • Advanced equipment: China has the highest number of proton therapy centers globally and leads in certain immunotherapies
  • Innovative treatments: China's CAR-T cell therapy programs have produced world-class outcomes at a fraction of Western costs
  • Comprehensive care: Major cities offer integrated treatment packages combining modern medicine with traditional Chinese medicine
  • Strategic location: Proximity to Southeast Asia, East Asia, and growing connectivity with Europe

The Hainan Boao Lecheng International Medical Tourism Pilot Zone represents China's flagship initiative in this space. This special economic zone offers experimental treatments and technologies not yet available elsewhere, attracting patients seeking cutting-edge options.

China's visa facilitation policies have also expanded, with medical visas now available for international patients and their families. Major hospitals in Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Chengdu have established dedicated international patient centers with multilingual staff and transparent pricing.

💡 Key Takeaways

What International Patients Should Know

  • India wins on price and English accessibility: For budget-conscious patients seeking common procedures, India's established medical tourism infrastructure offers excellent value with minimal language barriers.
  • China excels in complex treatments: For advanced cancer care, proton therapy, and innovative immunotherapies, China's leading hospitals rival global standards at significantly lower costs.
  • Both offer substantial savings: Whether choosing India or China, international patients can expect to pay 60-90% less than equivalent treatment in the US or Western Europe.
  • Geopolitical factors matter: Regional instability is redirecting patients from traditional Middle Eastern routes toward both India and China as stable, trusted alternatives.
  • China's moment is coming: With 73.6% growth in three years and increasing international accreditation, China is rapidly closing the gap with established competitors like India.
